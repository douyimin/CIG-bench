Abstract

Subsurface imaging understanding bridges observational geophysical data and quantitative geological models, underpinning applications ranging from hydrocarbon exploration and CO2 storage site evaluation to geological hazard monitoring. Over the past decade, deep learning has fundamentally transformed interpretation workflows, yet the field suffers from a critical infrastructure deficit: the absence of a unified, open, and reproducible benchmark has rendered fair method comparison nearly impossible, hindered cross-study reproducibility, and fragmented community efforts.

On the survey side, we systematically analyze 652 publications spanning 2015–2025, organized into four major task categories: structural interpretation (faults, horizons, RGT), geobody identification (channels, salt bodies, karst), seismic facies analysis, and property estimation (impedance, velocity, porosity). Citation-network analysis reveals asymmetric knowledge transfer patterns: structural interpretation serves as the methodological hub, geobody tasks act as cross-domain bridges, facies analysis links geometry to reservoir properties, and property estimation absorbs innovations from all domains while delivering the quantitative outputs most directly tied to industrial decisions.

As the central contribution of this article, we introduce CIG-Bench, an open-source benchmark library and evaluation platform designed not as a static dataset release but as a living, community-maintained infrastructure. CIG-Bench provides (1) pretrained baselines for fault segmentation, RGT estimation, geobody segmentation, and property modeling, validated across multiple real-world surveys; (2) standardized datasets with unified task definitions and evaluation protocols; (3) one-click inference and deployment APIs that dramatically lower the barrier for newcomers; and (4) a commitment to long-term maintenance, including continuous model updates and integration of community-contributed baselines.

An AI Framework for Subsurface Imaging

A structured overview of AI for geophysics-based subsurface imaging understanding, identifying core challenges and the AI-driven solutions that reshape existing research paradigms.

Figure 1. Two categories of core challenges are identified: inherent imaging limitations (blurriness, ambiguity, nonlinear mapping) and data characteristics (massive scale, heterogeneity, multi-scale features). AI-driven solutions—deep learning-based perception, probabilistic modeling, physics-informed learned mapping, efficient computing, multimodal fusion, and scale-aware learning—reshape existing research paradigms. Three open challenges define the frontier: interpretation under complex geological conditions, cross-domain semantic generalization, and benchmark dataset construction.

Key Contributions

1

Decade-spanning survey of 652 publications across four subsurface interpretation directions, with citation-network analysis that reveals cross-domain knowledge flow and methodological evolution from seismic attributes to domain foundation models.

2

Systematic task-wise review of deep-learning methods across structure, geobody, facies, and property tasks, tracing each evolutionary trajectory and identifying the remaining bottlenecks for cross-survey generalization.

3

CIG-Bench open-source library: pretrained baselines for fault, RGT, channel, karst, and property tasks with cross-survey transferability, plus one-click pip install inference APIs that load weights automatically from ModelScope or Hugging Face.

4

Long-term, community-maintained ecosystem: standardized splits and metrics, continuous model updates, and a roadmap for integrating community-contributed baselines and new tasks as the field evolves.

Interpretation Tasks

Subsurface interpretation is decomposed into four conceptually overlapping but methodologically distinct categories.

🏗️

Structure

Faults, horizons, unconformities, and relative geologic time (RGT) for constructing structural frameworks and stratigraphic models.

🪨

Geobody

3D segmentation of salt bodies, channels, karst cavities, and igneous intrusions with relatively independent geometries.

📊

Facies

Classification of seismic units by amplitude, frequency, continuity, and stratification — reflecting depositional environments.

🔬

Property

Inversion of impedance, velocity, porosity, density, and Vp from seismic data calibrated with sparse well logs.

Evolution of Research Paradigms

Click tabs or use arrows to explore the decade-long evolution of each task category.

Figure 5 · Structure. Seismic attributes (2015–16) → ML penetration with 2D segmentation and patch-based classification (2017–18) → synthetic-data era and 3D deep segmentation (FaultSeg3D, 2019–20) → semi-/weak supervision (2021–23) → domain foundation models (GEM/SAM, 2024–25).
Figure 6 · Geobody. Multiscale attribute enhancement → TGS Salt Challenge and supervised 2D segmentation → 3D end-to-end volumetric segmentation with synthetic and semi-supervised training → SAM-style prompting and unified foundation frameworks (GEM).
Figure 7 · Facies. Attribute-based clustering and SOM → deep feature clustering and contrastive learning → self-supervised pretraining and cross-survey generalization with horizon-based and sectional viewing paradigms.
Figure 8 · Property. Physics-driven inversion (Bayesian / geostatistical) → end-to-end deep inversion → foundation models (GEM) → cross-survey generalisation with unified seismic + well-log conditioning.

CIG-Bench Synthetic Dataset

A unified synthetic corpus paired with ground-truth labels for all five tasks under controlled, geologically reasonable conditions.

Figure 9. Representative samples from the CIG-Bench synthetic dataset. Rows (a, b) show seismic samples with structural annotations — raw seismic amplitude volumes, fault labels with diverse fault styles, and relative-geologic-time volumes capturing folds, unconformities, and tilted strata. Rows (c, d) show seismic samples with geobody annotations including channel and karst-cave systems of varying scales, geometries, and spatial distributions. All samples are additionally paired with acoustic-impedance and velocity volumes (not shown). Every volume shares a uniform dimension of 512 × 512 × 1024 with lateral spacing 25 m × 25 m and vertical sampling 5 m.

Quick Start

One-line install. Five predictors. Weights downloaded automatically on first call.

pip install cig_bench
🏗️ Fault segmentation
from cig_bench.predictor.fault import FaultPredictor

fault_predictor = FaultPredictor(device="cuda")
prob, used = fault_predictor.predict(
    seis,
    rank=4, chunk_size=64,      # memory-bounded inference
    threshold=0.5,
    scale_t=0.5, scale_h=0.85, scale_w=0.85,
    resize_back=True,           # return result at the original (T,H,W)
)
fault_predictor.visualize(used, prob)
⏱️ Relative Geologic Time (RGT) estimation
from cig_bench.predictor.rgt import RGTPredictor

rgt_predictor = RGTPredictor(device="cuda")
rgt_vol, used = rgt_predictor.predict(seis)
horizons     = rgt_predictor.extract_horizons(rgt_vol, n_horizons=100)
rgt_predictor.visualize(used, rgt_vol, horizons)
🪨 Geobody segmentation (channel / karst)
from cig_bench.predictor.channel import ChannelPredictor

channel_predictor = ChannelPredictor(device="cuda")
scores, used = channel_predictor.predict(
    seis,
    scales=[0.5, 0.75, 1.0, 1.25, 1.5],   # custom scale set
    accumulate="sum",
)
mask = channel_predictor.postprocess(scores, threshold=0.75, min_size=50000)
channel_predictor.visualize(used, scores, mask)

# Karst follows an identical interface: only the checkpoint changes.
from cig_bench.predictor.karst import KarstPredictor
karst_predictor = KarstPredictor(device="cuda")
🔬 Property modeling (GEM-style conditional)
import numpy as np
from cig_bench.predictor.property import PropertyPredictor

prop_predictor = PropertyPredictor(device="cuda")
vp_vol, used, wells = prop_predictor.predict(
    seis, vp_log,
    infer_shape=(640, 512, 512),
)
prop_predictor.visualize(used, vp_vol, wells)

Weights download automatically on first use from ModelScope (default) or Hugging Face Hub.
Switch globally with export CIG_BENCH_WEIGHT_SOURCE=huggingface, or per-predictor via source="huggingface".
